/ Why This Is In My Portfolio

Fragrance Castle isn't an engineering project — but it's where I learned most of what I know about building things people actually use.

Running operations at the family business since 2019 has shaped how I approach product engineering. It taught me to think about cost, manufacturability, lead times, and real-world failure modes — not just the elegant technical solution. The packing terminal project (SwiftPack) came directly out of bottlenecks I lived through here.

/ The Context

Fragrance Castle is a Singapore-based retailer of perfumes and incense burners. The business existed before I joined operations — but it had no e-commerce presence and no wholesale arm.

Singapore's perfume and home-fragrance market is dominated by a handful of large players. Many of the brands we carry had never been formally introduced to the local market.

/ What I Did

Launched e-commerce across three platforms

Helped set up the company's Lazada, Shopee, and Shopify storefronts — product photography, listing copy, pricing strategy, and ongoing operations. Each platform has its own quirks, fee structures, and customer expectations.

Built the wholesale arm from scratch

Identified and onboarded new B2B customers across Singapore, Malaysia, and Indonesia. Many of our brand introductions had no prior SEA market presence — so it was a question of cold outreach, sampling, and building trust with buyers who'd never heard of these brands.

Day-to-day operations

  • Catalogue management across 200+ SKUs and 6 brands
  • Supplier coordination on stock levels, lead times, and quality
  • Order fulfilment, packing, customer service
  • Pricing decisions based on platform fees and competitor analysis

/ What This Taught Me About Engineering

Three things that translate directly to product development:

  • Real users don't behave like spec sheets. The way customers, suppliers, and partners actually use a system rarely matches how you designed it. Build in flexibility for that.
  • Bottlenecks aren't where you think they are. The slowest part of order fulfilment wasn't packing — it was logging into seller portals. The smallest, most invisible friction often dominates the total cost.
  • Cost matters more than cleverness. A solution that costs 10× more isn't 10× better. The constraint of running a small business teaches you to design within real budgets — which is exactly what product engineering requires.
